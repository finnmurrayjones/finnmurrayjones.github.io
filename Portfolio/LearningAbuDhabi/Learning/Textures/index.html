In much the same way as the buildings that surround them, Abu Dhabi’s public parks are also governed by the same overarching vision. The parks contain interesting examples of street furniture, mosques, public bathrooms, and service buildings, mostly designed in-house by the Municipality. Through their built forms, they represent repositories of information, a record of the decisions made that served the creation of a cohesive aesthetic identity, suitable for that of a capital city. The care taken and attention to detail present is remarkable.

Choices of materials reflect inherent value judgments. By comparing two eras of urbanism, we can thus track changing aesthetic preferences. Khalidiya Park, along with the Old Airport Garden and the Capital Garden (once known as Al Asima Public Garden), is one of Abu Dhabi’s earliest parks, present on maps from the late 1970s. It represents a so-called “first wave” of design. Khalifa Park, constructed in the 2000s at the other end of the island, represents a much more recent interpretation of this design ethos.
